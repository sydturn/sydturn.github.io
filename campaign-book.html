GREYHAVEN
A Campaign of Cozy Winter Horror
"The warmth of a fire. The smell of bread. A town full of people who do not yet know they are afraid."
For 3 Players · Early Winter · Mountain Region
Frontmatter
Campaign Overview
A guide for the Dungeon Master before the first candle is lit

Greyhaven is a campaign of contrasts. It asks the Dungeon Master to build something beautiful before they break it — to pour warmth and character into a small mountain town until the players genuinely love it, and only then to let the cold seep in.

The horror of this campaign is not the horror of monsters in the dark. It is the horror of wrongness. Of small things being slightly off. A wolf whose leg twitches after death. A dead man who can still knock on glass. A child who draws figures no one else can see. The world of Greyhaven does not scream its dread. It whispers it.

Campaign Themes

  • The warmth of belonging — The players must come to love this town and its people before anything threatens them. That love is the argument the campaign is making.
  • Death that does not rest — The central supernatural horror is an instability in the boundary between planes. The dead do not always stay dead — and some of those who return are not what they were.
  • The question of what is real — The campaign's deepest threat is not a monster but an idea: that everything the players love may be a false world, a dream built around a deeper truth. They must decide whether to fight for it anyway.
  • The fragment resonance — The player characters carry something that weakens the boundary between planes wherever they go. They are drawn together, and together they are dangerous.
  • Winter as metaphor — The unnatural storm is a symptom of planes bleeding into each other. It is death-cold leaking through cracks in reality. Treat it as a living, creeping presence.

A Note on Pacing

Dungeon Master's Note

The single most important rule of this campaign is this: do not rush to the horror. For every moment of dread you place before your players, give them two moments of warmth first. The horror only lands because they care. Your job in the early sessions is not to frighten — it is to make them love a place that will eventually be in danger. Build the warmth. Build the attachment. The rest will follow.

The Fragment Resonance

The three player characters are connected by something that was done to them — something implanted, something that has been growing. Each carries a planar anchor: a fragment of constructed magic embedded in them by a cult called the Choir of Last Mercy. The fragments weaken the boundary between planes wherever the carriers go. They are drawn together because together the resonance is stronger, and the cracks they create are wider.

They do not know any of this yet. They know only a shared vision when the storm first hits: three silhouettes standing together in falling snow.

The Central Mystery

Something is wrong with the boundary between planes. In Greyhaven it manifests as the boundary between the material world and the Shadowfell thinning — the dead returning wrong, arriving with something other than their own soul, or not arriving at all. Elsewhere in the world the same process is happening wherever the planes touch. The cult of the Choir of Last Mercy is doing this deliberately, and the party is part of how they are doing it. The players will see the edges of this before they understand its shape. That is exactly right.

How to Describe the Wrongness

When the supernatural intrudes, do not announce it. Slip it into the description as if it were an ordinary detail, then move on. Let the players catch it. Let them ask about it. If they don't, let it sit. It will accumulate.

Technique

Describe three normal things, then one wrong thing, then continue. "The wind picks up. Your cloaks pull sideways. The fire in the window ahead flickers. One of the wolves near the treeline does not blink." Then move on. Do not linger. Do not emphasize. The players will feel it.

What the Players Should Feel

At the end of Session One, the players should feel: "I liked that town. I want to go back. Something is wrong and I don't know what."

They should not feel: scared. Not yet. Unsettled. Curious. Fond of the people they just met. That is enough.

The Rule of the Crawl: Horror that sprints loses its teeth. Let it crawl. Let it be a smell before it is a shape. Let it be a shape before it is a sound. Let it be a sound before it becomes something with a name.

Session One · Scene One
The Road
Fragment resonance, the unnatural storm, and a meeting on the road

Each player character is traveling alone toward Greyhaven when the storm arrives. Run this opening briefly and individually — a sentence or two for each character's situation — then bring the resonance.

Describing the Storm

The snow came too fast. There was no warning — one moment the sky was the dull grey of an overcast afternoon, the next it was white and falling thick and heavy, the flakes too large, too dense, moving in a way that felt less like weather and more like intention. Stranger still: there is no sound. You cannot hear the wind. You cannot hear your own footsteps. The snow swallows everything. And somewhere in the silence, at the very edge of hearing, you think you can make out voices — not words, just voices — carried in air that should be empty.

The Fragment Resonance

Before the characters see each other, each one experiences the resonance alone. Deliver this privately if possible — a quick note passed, or a moment of quiet at the table — then return to the group.

Something happens in your chest. A pulse — not your heartbeat, but something beneath it. The world tips sideways. For a moment you are not on a road in a snowstorm. You are somewhere else, somewhere white and cold and vast, and there are three figures standing in the snow ahead of you. Silhouettes. Still. Watching. Then the dizziness passes and you are on the road again, and the snow is falling, and there is something ahead of you on the path.
Dungeon Master's Note

Do not explain the resonance. Do not give it a name. When the players ask what it was, have their characters feel that they don't know — only that it mattered. The recognition they feel when they see each other should be played as an instinct, not an explanation.

The Meeting

The characters see each other through the storm. Give them a moment to react. They are strangers. The recognition is uncanny and unasked for. Let them sit in the awkwardness. Let them decide whether to speak first, or wait, or bristle. Do not rush past this moment — it is the foundation of everything.

Then the storm makes the decision for them.

The Mechanic: Forced Together

Visibility drops to 10 feet. Characters who move farther than that from the group encounter the following:

The wind finds your ears. It is not wind. It is voices — dozens of them, overlapping, screaming things you cannot make out, close enough to feel hot breath that cannot be there. The road disappears. Every direction looks the same. You are not sure which way you came from.

A character who fails a DC 12 Wisdom saving throw is frightened until they return to within 10 feet of another character. They must stay together to reach town.

Dungeon Master's Note

This mechanic should feel more mysterious than punishing. The players will understand — even enjoy — being nudged together. Frame it as the world making a decision for them, not as a rail. They are bound by something older than this road.
Session One · Scene Two
The Lantern Rest
Arrival in Greyhaven, the inn, and first meetings

Arriving at The Lantern Rest Inn

The inn appears through the snow like a lantern dropped from a high window — warm, amber, impossible to miss. The sign above the door creaks on its chain. Inside, the world changes entirely. Fire. Voices. The smell of something with fat dripping into coals and bread cooling on a sill. Every table is full. Someone somewhere is playing a fiddle badly and loudly and nobody seems to mind. The storm does not exist in here.

Let the players breathe. Let them order food. Do not push the plot. The Lantern Rest is the beating heart of Greyhaven, and the players need to feel that before they feel anything else.

Mira Feld

She does not wait for an introduction. Before the players have fully sat down, bread lands on the table in front of them — slapped down with the efficiency of someone who has been feeding cold travelers for thirty years.

"You look like ghosts. Eat first, explain later."

Mira is the first person who claims them. She will remember their orders without asking twice. She will refill things before they're empty. She is not warm in a soft way — she is warm in the way a forge is warm, practical and reliable and a little scorching if you stand too close. The players should feel, immediately, that this person is on their side.

NPC Introductions — Running the Room

The following NPCs enter or reveal themselves naturally over the course of the meal. Do not introduce them all at once. Let there be gaps for the players to talk to each other, to look around, to exist in the space. These moments should feel discovered, not delivered.

Dungeon Master's Note

Run the NPC introductions like improv — follow the players' attention. If someone looks toward the fire, that's when Davan says something. If someone watches the door, that's when Tomm bursts through it. Let the players lead. The NPCs will find them.

See the Notable Persons section in the sidebar for full guidance on running each NPC. Their individual entries contain their full voice, their specific lines, and their long-term narrative role.

The Quest Board

The innkeeper — or Mira herself — mentions offhandedly that the storm has stranded people and a few folks in town need some help, if anyone's looking for something to do. There's no pressure. It's practical small-town talk. Three matters come up in conversation:

The Broken Cart
Quest Giver: Davan Creel, Blacksmith

A supply cart is stuck on the road outside town. Good opportunity to build a connection with Davan. Combat likely.

Find Moss
Quest Giver: Branik Halvyr, Hunter

A hunter's old dog didn't return from the woods. Wholesome at first. The payoff comes much later.

The Mushroom Ring
Quest Giver: Children in the corner

Kids whispering about a fairy ring in the forest. Brief. Deeply strange. Useful for Petal specifically.

Let the players choose. All paths lead back to town. All paths carry one small wrong note hidden inside them.

Session One · Final Scene
What the Window Shows
The session's closing horror beat

Run this late in the session, when the energy has wound down and the players are comfortable. The fire is lower. The inn is quieter. Most people have gone to bed. This is the moment.

The Knock

Choose one player — ideally the one sitting nearest a window, or the one who would be last to sleep. A sound.

Soft. Three times. Not a branch — too deliberate. Not the wind — too patient. Something is knocking on the glass.

When they look:

A man is standing in the snow outside the window. He is not dressed for winter. He is standing very still, the way something stands still when standing still is all it can manage. His face is pale in a way that has nothing to do with cold — it is the pale of stopped blood, of something that has been outside longer than tonight. He is looking directly at you. His mouth moves. You cannot hear the words through the glass, but you can read them, barely, as he presses closer. "It hurts to stay." Then he falls. Not backward, not sideways — he simply folds and disappears into the snow.

The Inspection

If the players go outside to find him:

He is lying face-down in the snow. When you turn him over, his face is slack and cold — not the cold of a man who has been standing in a storm, but the cold of something that has had days to settle. The skin at his fingertips has the gray-blue of a corpse kept too long. Whatever he was, he was not alive when he knocked on that window.

Any character with medical knowledge, or who makes a DC 10 Wisdom (Medicine) check, can confirm: this man has been dead for at least three days. His hands are bruised at the knuckles. From knocking.

Do not explain this. Do not have the NPCs explain it. Let it sit like a stone dropped in still water. End the session here, or very shortly after. Give the players the ride home with this image in their heads. That is where it belongs.

End Session One.

Session Two · Scene One
The Festival Night
Warmth, music, and a child's strange art

The festival is everything Tomm promised. Run it generously. Let the players enjoy themselves without agenda. There is food. There is warmth. There is music. Sister Maren is dancing, badly, with complete commitment. Davan is not dancing and looks like he would rather be anywhere else, which is exactly where he wants to be. Petal dances — let her have this, let it be genuine and unguarded. Ardikan is drinking with the careful focus of someone who has decided to participate in a human custom and is doing it correctly. Old Rikka is in a corner telling a story to two children who may or may not be following it.

The Lantern Rest has been rearranged — tables pushed to walls, lanterns hung low, something with strings being played with more enthusiasm than skill in the corner. The smell of mulled wine and roasting meat fills the room like a physical presence. Outside the windows, the snow is still falling. In here it might as well be midsummer.

Visage's Performance

Tomm has been waiting for this all night. At some point during the festival — after enough food, enough warmth, enough of the town feeling real — he plants himself in front of Visage and refuses to move until there is a performance.

If Visage agrees:

The room turns. Not all at once — it happens the way a crowd turns toward anything worth watching, gradually, without anyone deciding to. The music from the corner stops. Tomm is grinning so hard it looks painful. Sister Maren laughs harder than anyone else in the room — a real laugh, the kind that has no dignity in it. Mira, from behind the bar, throws a bread roll in what you understand to be celebration.
Dungeon Master's Note

This scene is not optional flavor. The party needs to perform for this town — to give something to it, not just receive from it — before the horror begins. That exchange matters. It is emotional collateral. You will spend it later. Do whatever it takes to make this moment happen.

Introducing Elsie

At some point during the festival, the players become aware of a small girl sitting alone at a table in the corner. She has a piece of charcoal and a folded piece of parchment. She is watching the party with the focused, uncomplicated attention of a child doing something important.

If they approach, or when enough time has passed, she approaches them. She sets the drawing on the table in front of them.

She has drawn them. Their likenesses, captured with startling accuracy for a child her age. It is a sweet moment. Thank her. Move on. The drawing goes wherever drawings go — a pocket, a pack, the table.

· · ·

Later in the session — after a quest, after an hour, after they've forgotten about it — one of the players looks at the drawing again.

She has added something. Behind each figure in the drawing, there are tall shapes — narrow, masked, slightly wrong in their proportions. They stand very close. They were not there before.

If they ask Elsie about the figures:

She looks at the drawing and then up at you with an expression that suggests she cannot understand why you're confused about something so obvious. "They've always been there," she says. Then she goes back to her parchment.
Dungeon Master's Note

Elsie is not malevolent. She is simply able to see something that others cannot. Do not make her sinister — she should remain a perfectly ordinary, slightly eerie child. The horror is not in her. It is in what she draws.
Quest · Session One
The Broken Cart
A simple errand. A wolf that doesn't stay dead.

The Situation

Three days before the storm arrived, Davan sent his apprentice — a boy named Edwick, seventeen, generally reliable — to collect a supply cart from the timber merchant in the next valley over. The cart was loaded with iron stock, charcoal bricks, and two crates of tools Davan had ordered before winter set in. Edwick made it as far as the Ashford road before the storm came down faster than anyone expected. He unhitched the horse, tied off the cart, and walked back to town with the animal. Sensible enough.

The cart has been sitting on the Ashford road for two days. Davan cannot leave it there — the iron will rust under standing snow, and the tools are not replaceable until spring thaw opens the passes again. He intended to go himself, but his knee has been bad since the cold hit, and the road is a mile out. He does not say any of this to the party. He says:

"Supply cart. Ashford road, mile out, past the split pine. Wheel's stuck in a ditch. I need the cargo if not the cart. You'll be paid."

He pauses, then adds, as if it costs him something:

"I'll come with you."
Dungeon Master's Note

Davan offering to come is significant. He does not ask for company. He does not trust easily. If the players pick up on the fact that he's limping slightly on the way out, he will not acknowledge it if asked. He simply keeps walking. Let him keep up. Don't make it a scene.

The Cart

It is exactly where Edwick left it — a two-wheeled flat cart, tilted hard to one side where the right wheel dropped into a frozen drainage ditch. The cargo is still there, covered in canvas that has since frozen stiff with snow. One of the canvas corners has torn loose and the crate beneath is packed with ice.

Getting the cargo free requires either:

  • Strength DC 11 — Rock the cart out of the ditch well enough to roll it back to town
  • Or simply unload the cargo by hand — no check, just time and cold hands

Davan will work alongside them without comment. He stacks iron stock with the efficiency of someone who has been moving heavy things his entire life.

Into the Forest

The forest outside town is not the forest it should be. It is too quiet — not the quiet of peace, but the quiet of something holding its breath. There are no birds. There is no wind in the branches. There is only the snow, still falling, and your footsteps, which feel too loud in all of this stillness.

The Wolves

The party encounters three wolves on the road near the cart. These are ordinary wolves — hungry, territorial, doing exactly what wolves do in a bad winter. They circle. They snarl. They test for weakness. One lunges first, the others follow. Run this as a clean, normal combat encounter.

Dungeon Master's Note — First Encounter

Play these wolves completely straight. No hesitation, no unnatural stillness, no wrong silence. They are wolves. They behave like wolves. The players should feel like capable adventurers who handled a real threat. The only strangeness here comes after — not during. The contrast between normal wolves doing normal things and what happens when one dies is exactly the point. Don't muddy it.

Until one of them dies.

The wolf drops into the snow. Its body goes still. You're looking at something else when it happens — the leg. It twitches. Once. The body shifts, just slightly, the weight redistributing as though something is trying to find its feet from inside. Then it stops. The wolf is still. The snow falls on its fur.
Dungeon Master's Note

Nothing else happens. If the players investigate, the wolf is dead — fully, completely dead. There is nothing to find. Whatever tried to happen, didn't. Move on as naturally as you can. The players will carry this with them whether you dwell on it or not.

Davan's Moment

After combat, as they're loading the cart, Davan looks at Ardikan. A long moment. Then:

"You fight like someone who doesn't want to."

He lets that sit.

"That's good."

He doesn't explain what he means. He goes back to checking the cart wheel. That's all. That's enough.

Reward

  • Payment from Davan (modest, given without ceremony)
  • Davan's Trust — he will now speak to them with slightly more words
Quest · Session Two
Find Moss
A hunter's old dog. A frozen creek. A long-term payoff.

Branik Halvyr is a man who handles his own problems. Asking for help costs him something. He approaches the party quietly, away from the crowd, and keeps his eyes on the middle distance while he speaks.

"My dog Moss didn't come back from the woods last night. Storm's coming harder and he's too old for that."

A pause.

"I'll pay if you bring him back."

If the players hesitate, Tomm appears from nowhere:

"Moss is the best dog in town!"

What Branik Tells Them

  • Moss is old, but smart — smarter than most dogs Branik has known
  • He was following a deer trail toward Frost Creek
  • He has never once failed to return before dark
  • Quietly: "He's never gotten lost before."

Travel to Frost Creek

The forest swallows sound. Your footsteps vanish into the snow before they've finished happening. No birds have called since you left the treeline. The only tracks you can find are old ones — paws, small and regular, moving east.

Survival DC 10 — Follow Moss's trail. On success, the tracks reveal pawprints and, closer to the creek, drag marks in the snow where he struggled.

The Frozen Creek

The creek has frozen solid, covered by a thin skin of fresh snow. Beneath the ice, dark shapes move in the slow current — just debris, just current. But there is a sound. Faint. Exhausted. Whining. It comes from the center of the creek, where a section of ice has fractured around something dark and still and alive.

Moss's hind leg broke through thin ice. The current pinned him beneath a shelf. He is alive, but barely, and too tired to struggle anymore.

Petal's Moment

If Petal is the one who approaches Moss, even before he is freed:

The whining stops. He turns his head toward you — not the way a panicking animal turns, but the way an old dog turns when someone familiar has entered a room. His tail, barely, begins to move.

The Strange Moment

When Moss is finally pulled free, warm him up, check him over — and then, one player who makes a Perception DC 12 check notices:

Moss goes still. His ears flatten against his skull. He is looking into the woods across the creek, at a point between two dark trees. He growls — low, controlled, the growl of a dog that has found something and knows what it is. Nothing is there. After a moment, he looks away.

Optional Discovery

Near the creek, partially buried in snow, there is a small fragment of bone — carved, shaped, bearing marks that look deliberate. An Arcana or Religion DC 13 check reveals only: it feels wrong. Like something that was part of a ritual that should not have been performed. Do not elaborate.

The Return

Branik kneels beside Moss for a long moment. Scratches behind his ears. Doesn't speak. Then he stands and gives the party a single short nod.

"You did good."

Reward: A small coin pouch. An open offer of venison when the hunting improves. Branik's quiet, particular respect.

Long-Term Payoff: When Branik eventually dies, Moss returns to town alone. He refuses to look at anyone. He sits facing the forest — not sleeping, not resting, just watching the treeline, hour after hour, as if waiting for something to emerge from it.
Quest · Session Two
The Quiet Wolves
A hunting trip. Death that doesn't take. The first real crack.

With the storm deepening, the town needs fresh meat. Calder Voss — experienced hunter, quiet and capable — approaches the party. He needs extra hands. He doesn't say please, but he says it the way people who never say please say it.

"Storm's killing our traps. Need fresh meat before supplies run dry." He glances at each of you. "You'll do."

Into the Forest

The deeper you go, the stranger the silence becomes. You pass a place where deer tracks simply stop — mid-stride, in the open, with nothing around them. There are birds on branches above, sitting perfectly still. Their eyes are open. Calder doesn't look up. He's following something. Wolf tracks. Fresh ones, leading into a stand of dark pines.

The Wolf Pack

Three wolves emerge from the tree line. If the players remember the wolves from Session One, they will expect snarling, circling, a lunge. That is not what happens.

Dungeon Master's Note — Second Encounter

This is the same creature as before — same stats, same threat — but something has been taken out of it and something else put in its place. These wolves have been touched by whatever is wrong with the land. They are not feral. They are not hunting. They are watching. If your players had the first encounter fresh in mind, the absence of growling will land harder than any monster description. Let the silence do it.
They do not growl. They do not circle. They walk toward you with a directness that has nothing of the animal in it — no posturing, no warning. They simply watch you, coming closer, as if curious about something that should not be possible.

Combat proceeds normally. The wolves fight with ordinary wolf statistics. Kill them, wound them, drive them back — whatever the players decide.

The Horror Moment

When the first wolf is killed:

The wolf drops. Its body settles into the snow. You watch it. It is still. Then its front leg moves — slowly, with great effort, the motion of something that has forgotten how bodies work. The leg pushes. The body shifts. It is trying to stand. It cannot. It lies still. The snow falls on its side and does not melt.

Calder crouches over the corpse. He does not speak for a moment.

"That wasn't right." He examines the body. Looks up. "Animal's been dead at least a day."

Ardikan's Moment

If Ardikan places a hand on the wolf's body:

There is something still in there — not thought, not intention, just a residue of something that was. The word that finds you is not a word. It is a feeling. Confusion. Then nothing.

The Detail That Stays

After the fight, after everything has settled, one player makes a Perception DC 13 check:

The wolf's nose. You're looking at the dead wolf, already thinking about the walk back, when you notice it. The air above its muzzle fogs. Just once. One small, pale breath rising in the cold, and then nothing. Then nothing. Then nothing.
Dungeon Master's Note

Do not explain this. Do not repeat it. If players ask Calder, he didn't see it. Move on. It is a stone dropped in still water. The ripples are the point.

The Return and Evening's End

The town receives the meat warmly. Mira cooks stew. The fire is high. The mood lifts. But before the party turns in, Calder finds them — privately, away from the warmth.

"Something's wrong in these woods." He doesn't say anything else.

Optional: The Sound Beneath the Ground

That night, if any player is awake late enough:

Wolves are howling somewhere in the dark. But the sound is wrong — muffled, as if the howls are coming from underground. From beneath the forest floor, not from creatures moving through it.

Then silence. Then sleep.

Quest · Session One (Optional)
The Mushroom Ring
A short, strange scene. Particularly useful for Petal.

This is not a full quest — it is a scene, brief and strange, that the party may pass through on their way back from another errand. Children in the corner of the inn were whispering about a fairy ring they saw in the forest. If the players investigated, or if they simply pass near it:

The ring of mushrooms sits in a small clearing, dusted with snow that has not quite covered the caps. They form a near-perfect circle, dark brown and frost-edged, about ten feet across. The trees around them are leaning slightly inward. You did not notice that until you were looking.

If Petal Steps Inside

Something in your chest seizes. The magic — whatever lives in you that was never properly taught to sit still — wakes all at once. Frost creeps along the mushroom caps, radiating from your feet. You can hear something at the very edge of speech — Sylvan, you think, but spoken wrong, spoken by something that learned the words and not the meaning. At the edge of the ring, a dead rabbit. Its hind legs are moving.

A DC 14 Charisma saving throw is required to control the surge. On failure, a small magical effect occurs — nothing harmful, but very visible. On success, the feeling passes.

Then: stillness. The mushrooms are still. The rabbit is still. The frost retreats. Walk out of the ring and keep moving.

Dungeon Master's Note

The mushroom ring is a natural ley point where the boundary between life and death is already thin — not because of the cult, but because this land has always been strange here. Petal's wild magic responds to it instinctively. It is also the first time the resurrection wrongness presents itself in something as small as a dead rabbit.

Character Summary

Petal is a princess of the western fairy kingdom, exiled after she accidentally turned her fiancé into a toad with an uncontrolled surge of wild magic. Ideals, Bonds & Flaws

  • Flaw: She follows any toad she sees, no matter what is happening. She becomes distracted by memory at the worst moments. The Secret

The mushroom necklace she wears is the cure for her fiancé. She has never told anyone this. She will not tell anyone this until she trusts them completely, and perhaps not even then.

A Horror Moment Specifically for Petal

At some point she will find a toad staring at her with human eyes. It will whisper her name: "Petal?" But it is not her fiancé. Do not explain what it is. Do not explain why it knew her name.

The Fragment Inside Her

Petal's fragment resonates with the Feywild boundary. The cult found her because her wild magic accident at home — the day she turned her fiancé into a toad — cracked the planar boundary at that location in the Feywild. The cult noticed the crack. They planted the fragment in the damage she had already caused. She does not know this. She carries guilt for what she did to her fiancé; she does not yet know she also cracked open a piece of the world doing it, and that the cult was watching.

The fragment merged with her emotions rather than her magic directly. This is why her power surges when she feels strongly — her emotional state directly affects the planar boundary near her. When she is at peace, the boundary holds. When she grieves, or rages, or loves too hard, it thins.

Fragment Symptoms (Early)

  • Flowers frost over when she cries
  • Mushrooms grow where she sleeps — not ordinary ones

Character Summary

Ardikan was raised by a cult that worshipped an evil god. As a rite, he was thrown into a pit filled with venomous creatures — a Gu pot, designed to produce a single survivor of supernatural resilience. He survived. The others did not. The experience broke whatever the cult wanted to make and produced something else instead: a man enlightened by the true god Padhra, carrying both the techniques of death-meditation and a quiet horror at having lived when others didn't. He wanders, spreads his faith, and works to dismantle the evil he was raised inside.

The Secret He Carries

In the Gu pit, many died. Sometimes he dreams of them whispering: "You were supposed to die with us." He wonders, in the quiet hours, whether he stole their chance at peace by surviving. Padhra teaches that death is peace. Ardikan is not always sure he believes he deserved to reach it.

The Fragment Inside Him

Ardikan's fragment resonates with the death boundary — the threshold between the living world and the Fugue Plane. The cult found him because he had already crossed it. He died in the Gu pit. He came back. That return left a seam in the boundary that the cult recognized and exploited: he is a man who has been through Kelemvor's gate and emerged on the wrong side of it, and the fragment was planted in the seam his survival created.

The cult selected him believing his death-meditation discipline would keep the fragment contained. They were partially right — it is contained. It is also integrated more deeply than any other vessel. His fragment does not just weaken the death boundary near him. It gives him genuine perception of what lies on the other side of it.

Fragment Symptoms (Early)

  • He hears the final thoughts of dying creatures — not words, just feeling
  • Shadows cling to him slightly longer than they should
  • Sometimes he knows, without knowing how, that someone near him will die soon
  • Occasionally, in quiet moments, he hears the Fugue: a distant gray silence that is almost peaceful. His god Padhra teaches that death is peace. The Fugue sounds like Padhra is right. This is the fragment's most insidious symptom.

Fragment Evolution — Merciful Silence

Ardikan can instantly end the suffering of dying creatures. The release is real and complete. But every time he uses it, the fragment cracks the death boundary a little wider at that point, and the soul departs not cleanly but through the wound. He may not know this for some time. His arc is partly about whether the mercy he gives is real mercy or just the fragment using him.

NPC Bonds

Ardikan connects most with: Davan Creel (two men shaped by violence into something quieter), Father Elric (theological curiosity that Ardikan finds unexpectedly moving), and Sister Maren (who asks about his god with the sincerity of someone who genuinely wants to understand). Dungeon Master's Note

The specific trap for Ardikan is the cult's theology: Padhra teaches that death is peace. The Fugue sounds exactly like peace. Character Summary

Visage is a warforged built for assassination and subterfuge. He communicates entirely non-verbally — sign language, written notes, and a bag of masks he carries to express emotions. After what he calls his awakening, he found a traveling circus of performers and thieves who taught him their ways. He loves to entertain. He is mischievous. He carries the masks the way other people carry faces.

The Secret He Doesn't Know

Visage was originally built by the Choir of Last Mercy. They used him to deliver fragments to vessels — carrying them across distances, placing them without the targets knowing. His memory of this has been erased. He does not know he was a weapon pointed at the people he now travels with. He does not know that the fragment inside him arrived there because he put it there himself.

When to Reveal This: This secret should not arrive all at once. Drip it. A flash of memory he can't explain. A route he knows without having traveled it. A name he recognizes that he shouldn't. Let him find the truth in pieces, and let each piece be worse than the last.

The Fragment Inside Him

Visage's fragment resonates with all planar barriers simultaneously — because Visage himself exists outside the systems that define them. He has no soul, which means Kelemvor has no record of him: he is a blank space in the ledger of the dead. He cannot die normally. He may be out of time, an anachronism moving through a present that his existence slightly destabilizes. The cult chose him specifically because a being outside the soul-cycle, carrying an anchor, would not trigger Kelemvor's awareness at all. He was the perfect invisible weapon.

What the cult did not anticipate: the fragment has been slowly building something. Not a soul — or not exactly. Something that is becoming a soul, accreting around the fragment like a pearl around an irritant. The fragment was supposed to weaken barriers. Instead it is creating something new inside a machine that was never supposed to hold it. This is unprecedented and the cult does not know it is happening.

The irony of Visage: the thing making him most human is the same thing the cult put in him to use him. And as it develops, Kelemvor will begin to notice a blank space in his ledger that is slowly, improbably, filling in.

Fragment Symptoms (Early)

  • Mirrors sometimes show a different expression than the one on his mask
  • His shadow moves half a second behind him
  • Animals whine softly near him — not in fear, in something like recognition
  • All planar barriers near him are slightly unstable — more so than near the other two

Fragment Evolution — Borrowed Faces

Visage can briefly speak in the voice of someone dead. He does not always choose to do this. Sometimes it happens on its own. Sometimes he says things he did not intend to say, in voices that are not his. As his developing soul grows, the dead are drawn to him — not with hostility, but with the confused recognition of people who sense something familiar inside something that should not be familiar.

The Living Dead and Visage

The soul-torn dead show little interest in Visage. They may approach and then stop. One might press its face against his chest, listening. Then say, quietly: "There's nothing." Then walk away. This should feel deeply wrong — both to the players and to Visage himself, who is no longer certain the dead are wrong.

NPC Bonds

Visage connects most with: Tomm (devoted, delighted, calls him a hero), Old Rikka (who treats him like a person without ever making it a statement), and Elsie (who draws his masks with the seriousness of someone documenting something important). Tomm's return and his silence is Visage's specific breaking point.

The Greyhaven Cemetery
The dead are supposed to stay here. They have begun to disagree.
Overview

The cemetery sits behind the chapel, surrounded by an iron fence that the town replaced twelve years ago when the old wooden one rotted through. It holds perhaps two hundred graves, the oldest dating to Greyhaven's founding. The newer plots are toward the eastern wall. The groundskeeper's small shed is near the gate. The groundskeeper quit four days ago without explanation. His tools are still in the shed. His coat is still on the hook.

The cemetery is not considered dangerous. It has always been a quiet, maintained place where people come to leave flowers and think. This perception is becoming incorrect.

What It Looks Like

Rows of stone markers, some old enough that the names have worn smooth. Evergreen bushes trimmed back for winter. Snow that lies undisturbed over most of the grounds — except near the eastern wall, where the snow is uneven, as though it has settled strangely around the plots there. Footprints in the snow lead to the gate and stop. Not from the gate inward. From inside, toward the gate, and then gone.

Points of Interest
  • The Sunken Plot (E-7). One grave on the eastern wall has subsided — the earth above it compressed, as if weight pressed down from below rather than above. The stone reads ORVYN FELD — BELOVED — HE KEPT THE LANTERN LIT. Mira's husband.
  • The Unmarked Section. Behind a screen of overgrown yew hedge, a small area of flat ground with no markers. DC 13 Investigation reveals disturbed earth in a circular pattern — old, but recently touched. This is where the old symbols-not-names graves were. Old Rikka knows this.
  • The Groundskeeper's Shed. His name was Aldric Bone. Inside the shed: tools, a logbook of burial records (useful for cross-referencing who has died recently), and a single page torn from it folded in his coat pocket. The page lists five names. Three of them are people the party has met. The entries are dated last week. None of them are dead yet.
  • The Eastern Wall at Night. DC 14 Perception after dark: the soil near the wall moves. Not dramatically. A slow, rhythmic shift, like breathing. Nothing emerges. Not yet.
DM Notes

The cemetery is the campaign's slow-burning centerpiece. Bring the party here early, let it feel solemn and ordinary. Let them find Orvyn's grave and connect it to Mira. Let them leave without incident.

Then, later, bring them back at night.

The names in Aldric's logbook should include at least one PC or a named NPC the party loves. Don't confirm what it means. Don't deny it. Let them carry it.

The unmarked section is where the Choir of Last Mercy performed their first rite. The soil remembers. The Soultorn Dragon's influence seeps upward here more than anywhere else in town.

Lore & Mysteries
The Fragments
What they are. Why the party carries them. What they are becoming.

What a Fragment Is

A fragment is a planar anchor — a piece of constructed ritual magic embedded into a living person. The cult of the Choir of Last Mercy has spent generations identifying, hunting, and implanting these fragments into individuals who have already crossed the boundaries between planes, using their existing connection to the boundary as a foundation. The fragment builds on the crack that is already there.

A fragment is not a parasite. It does not control. It integrates — slowly, quietly — with the deepest part of the vessel's nature. But its effect on the world is not quiet: wherever a fragment-bearer travels, the barriers between planes thin. The material world, the Feywild, the Shadowfell, the Fugue Plane — all of it becomes slightly more permeable around them. The fragments are not weapons. They are keys that are permanently stuck in the lock.

How the Party Was Chosen

The three player characters each had a crack in the boundary before the cult ever found them. The fragment was placed in the crack to widen it.

CharacterTheir CrossingWhat the Fragment Amplifies

ArdikanDied and was reborn; he has been through the death-boundary and returnedThe boundary between the living world and the Fugue weakens near him
VisageNo soul, cannot die normally, possibly out of time; exists outside the cycles that define all other beingsAll planar barriers near him become unstable — he is the anchor the cult is most afraid of and most excited about

Together, the three fragments resonate and compound. One alone is a slow leak. Three together, in proximity, is a wound the planes cannot close on their own.

Fragment Resonance

When the three fragments are in proximity, they resonate. The party experiences this as:

  • A sudden cold that comes from inside rather than outside
  • Whispering at the edge of hearing — not words, something older
  • The sense that the air around them is thinner, like being near an open window in winter
  • Occasionally: brief, flickering overlaps — a shadow that belongs to another plane, a flower that is wrong for this climate, the smell of earth that has never seen sunlight

Sometimes, when the resonance is strong, all three characters dream the same dream: a vast gray plain beneath a colorless sky. A city of pale stone. Thousands of figures, still and waiting, who all slowly turn to look at them.

The Terrible Truth

There are two terrible truths, and the party will learn them at different times.

The first truth — which they will learn when they begin to understand what the fragments are — is that removing a fragment kills the vessel. The fragment has integrated too deeply. It cannot be cut out cleanly. The party cannot simply be freed.

The second truth — which the cult will eventually use against them — is the ideological trap at the center of this campaign. The cult will tell them, patiently and with evidence, that the world they love is not real. That the material plane, the Feywild, the Shadowfell — all of it is a dream wrapped around the Fugue Plane, the only true world. That the friends they have made in Greyhaven are false versions of souls who deserve to exist in their true forms. The campaign asks: what do you do when someone makes a compelling argument that everything you love is an illusion? Do you fight for it anyway? Or does the idea take root?

The Real Horror: It is not the dead returning. It is not the cult. It is the moment a player character thinks — even for a second — what if they're right? Build toward that moment. Everything else is scaffolding.

Possible Paths to Deactivation

The party does not know how to deactivate their fragments. This is a question the campaign will slowly answer. Current possibilities — none confirmed, all dangerous:

  • Find and destroy the ritual sites — the cult created anchor-points in each plane where the fragments were prepared. Destroying these may sever the fragment's connection to the planar boundary without killing the vessel. Greyhaven's mass grave is one such site.
  • Dismantle the cult entirely — if the infrastructure that maintains the fragment network is gone, the fragments may go inert. This requires finding and defeating something the party has barely glimpsed.
  • Bring the fragments to Kelemvor — the god of death judges all souls. He may be able to recognize the fragments as the violations they are and remove them through divine authority. This path is dangerous: Kelemvor may simply choose to collect the vessels and end the risk entirely.
  • Find the other vessels — the party are not the only fragment-bearers in the world. Other people were chosen. Their choices — to fight, to accept, or to help the cult — will shape what options remain.
Lore & Mysteries
The Choir of Last Mercy
Their doctrine. Their hierarchy. Their masks.

The Doctrine

The Choir of Last Mercy believes that the Fugue Plane — the gray, still domain where all souls go after death, where they wait to be judged by Kelemvor — is the only real world. Everything else is illusion. The material plane, the Feywild, the Shadowfell, the outer planes — all of it, they teach, is a dream wrapped around the one true reality. A false narrative that souls are forced to live inside before they are finally allowed to wake up.

Their evidence is coherent and hard to dismiss: every soul, regardless of faith or origin, passes through the Fugue. Gods themselves must interact with it. Death reveals it. If the Fugue is where everything ends up — if it is the destination of every being that has ever existed — then it must be the base layer. The true world. And everything the living experience is the dream of someone who has not yet woken.

Life is a dream. Death is awakening. This is their creed.

They intend to collapse the false planes back into the Fugue — to end the dream and return all beings to their true forms. In their theology this is salvation. In reality it would destroy civilization, collapse planar barriers, and flood all of existence with death-energy. What remains would not be paradise. It would be the Fugue Plane, vast and gray and silent, holding every soul that ever was, with nowhere left to go.

The inside of every cultist's bone mask is carved with screaming faces. The outside is carved smooth and peaceful. This is intentional. This is their theology.

The Hierarchy

The Cantors
Field agents · Masked · Traveling

The Cantors are the face of the Choir that the party will encounter first. They travel the world, identify candidates, plant fragments, and observe vessels from a distance. Their bone masks have a particular property: they can be pressed against another face to steal that person's appearance for a limited time. This is how cult agents have moved through towns undetected — and it is why, for a time, the party may not be certain whether the dead are truly returning or whether masked agents are walking among them wearing borrowed faces.

The masks work because of a theological irony: the cult teaches that material identity is false, a dream-layer that Kelemvor does not judge. The masks rewrite material identity — how reality recognizes the wearer — which makes them effectively invisible to a god who judges by soul, not face. The cult believes the masks carry Kelemvor's blessing. The truth is stranger: the masks work because the cult's theology is partially correct. Kelemvor has no idea they exist.

A Cantor caught or confronted will be calm. They do not panic. They explain their mission with the serenity of absolute belief. One possible line, delivered without menace: "You've grown well."

The Vestry
High priests · Ritual overseers

The Vestry oversee permanent ritual sites — mass graves, severing chambers, soul anchors. They know where all fragment vessels are. The mass grave beneath Greyhaven was one of their sites. They performed the ritual slaughter there. They do not visit unless something goes wrong.

The Grand Requiem
The cult's leader · Unseen · Speaks through mirrors

No one has seen their face. They communicate through reflective surfaces — mirrors, still water, polished metal. Their voice is described by those who have heard it as: many voices trying to whisper at once. They know the party exists. When the Soultorn Dragon dies and the fragments flare, the Grand Requiem will know exactly where the party is.

The Mask Agents — A Note on Confusion

Dungeon Master's Note

The Cantor masks that allow identity theft are a deliberate tool of confusion. Introduce the idea of the dead returning before the party finds the masks. Let them assume the dead are coming back. Then let them find a mask and realize some of what they saw may have been agents. Then let them realize that some of it was genuinely dead people returning. The uncertainty is the point. Never fully resolve which encounters were which.
Lore & Mysteries
Kelemvor & the Fugue Plane
The god of death. The true world the cult wants to restore. The complication the party cannot avoid.

The Fugue Plane

When mortals die, their souls travel to the Fugue Plane — a vast, colorless domain of still gray light and featureless plains. Its only landmark is the City of Judgment, a pale and silent city where souls wait, sometimes for days, before divine servants collect them and carry them onward to their god's domain. The faithful are claimed. The Faithless are not.

Kelemvor rules from the Crystal Spire at the city's center. He judges each soul not by what they did, but by the intent behind it and the regret in their heart. He is direct, taciturn, and genuinely fair — not gentle, but just. He does not take pleasure in punishment. He is simply the last gate, and the gate must function.

Why the Cult Has a Point

The cult's theology is not pure delusion. The Fugue Plane does receive every soul. Gods do interact with it. Death does reveal it. The cult has identified something real: the Fugue is foundational to how existence is structured, and every other plane is, in some metaphysical sense, temporary.

What the cult has gotten wrong is the conclusion. The planes being built around the Fugue does not make them false. A dream is still experienced. The warmth of a fire in the Lantern Rest is still warmth. Sister Maren's kindness is still kindness. The cult's error is a philosophical one, but it is seductive, and the campaign should let it be seductive.

Dungeon Master's Note

When the cult eventually presents their argument directly to the party — clearly, patiently, with evidence — do not make it easy to dismiss. Let it land. Let the players actually wrestle with it. The point is not that the cult is obviously wrong. The point is that the party chooses to fight for their world even when they can't fully refute the argument against it.

Kelemvor's Awareness — and His Blindness

Kelemvor feels every disruption to the death cycle. Each soul that does not complete its journey, each body that returns wrong, each boundary that thins — these register to him as violations, and he responds through his normal channels: empowering clergy, sending doomguides, issuing omens through his priesthood. He knows something is wrong. He does not know who is doing it.

The cult's bone masks exploit the same loophole their theology describes. Kelemvor judges by soul, not by material identity. The masks rewrite material identity — the layer he does not watch. The cultists move through his domain undetected. He feels the wound. He cannot see the hand making it.

How Kelemvor Views the Party

From Kelemvor's perspective, the three party members are walking violations. They carry fragments that weaken the planar barriers that his domain depends on. Ardikan has already passed through the death boundary and returned — an anomaly Kelemvor's records show but cannot explain. Visage has no soul, which means Kelemvor has no record of him at all: he is a blank space in the ledger of the dead, which is deeply wrong. Petal's fragment resonates with the Feywild boundary in ways that create interference in the judgment process for fey souls.

Kelemvor is not the party's enemy. But he might kill them anyway — not out of malice, but because the pragmatic solution to a wound is to close it, and the party is the wound. He would not be wrong to do this. That is what makes him frightening.

Kelemvor — What the Party Can Use
The god of death is not an enemy. He is a constraint. And a potential answer.

If the party can find a way to bring their case to Kelemvor — to demonstrate that the cult is actively undermining the death cycle he is supposed to protect — he becomes the most powerful ally imaginable. He can remove fragments through divine authority in a way no mortal magic can. He can judge and release the souls trapped in the Greyhaven wound. He can pursue the cult across planes.

But there is no guarantee he would let the vessels survive the removal. And there is no guarantee he would agree that the material plane is worth saving, rather than simply accelerating the process the cult is attempting in a more controlled way. Kelemvor's interest is the integrity of the death cycle, not the preservation of false worlds.

Approaching him is a gamble. It may be the only gamble that wins.

Kelemvor and the Wall of the Faithless

One detail the party may eventually learn, which adds texture to the cult's argument: the Faithless — those who lived without genuine faith in any god — do not pass peacefully through the Fugue. They become part of the Wall of the Faithless, a living structure of compressed souls that slowly dissolves. This is canonical. The cult knows it. They point to it as evidence that the current system is broken — that souls suffer under Kelemvor's watch.

What the cult does not say is that their solution — collapsing all planes into the Fugue — would not free the Faithless. It would simply add everyone else to the wall.

Lore & Mysteries
The Soultorn Dragon
A tragedy chained below a mass grave. The campaign's central confrontation.

What It Was

Once, it was an ancient dragon — old enough that its name has been lost to everyone except the cult that captured it. The Choir used it as the anchor for their first attempt at planar collapse: a ritual designed to tear the boundary between the material plane and the Fugue simultaneously at a single point, forcing hundreds of souls through at once and cracking the barrier wide enough to begin the cascade they needed. The dragon was chosen because an ancient dragon's soul is vast enough to hold multiple planes in tension simultaneously. They tore its soul apart and used the pieces as anchors on each side of the boundary.

The ritual partially worked. The boundary cracked. The souls did not pass through cleanly — they got stuck, caught between the material world and the planes bleeding into it. The dragon did not die. It became the wound itself: a creature existing in multiple planes simultaneously, its torn soul holding the crack open simply by continuing to exist.

What It Is Now

It is enormous and wrong. Its skeletal wings are torn through with spirit-light — ghostly, flickering, the color of a candle seen through fog. Its ribs have cracked open from the inside, and within the cavity you can see a storm of shapes: human-like, reaching, cycling endlessly. Every breath it releases carries voices. Not roaring. Voices. Dozens of them, overlapping, and somewhere in the overlap you think you hear someone say: "Please stop."

The chamber it occupies is not fully in any one plane. The walls bleed — here the stone is material-solid, there it is Shadowfell-gray and cold, in one corner something grows that could only be Feywild. The dragon is the reason. Its existence is holding three planes partially merged at this location. When it dies, the planes will attempt to separate — violently.

Combat Features

  • Ghostly humanoid forms peel off its body during combat and attack independently
  • The ground beneath it bleeds spectral energy — difficult terrain, psychic damage
  • It occasionally speaks in multiple voices simultaneously; one voice may say something coherent and devastating
  • Soul anchors placed around the chamber must be destroyed to weaken it — the party must split focus between the dragon and the anchors

After the Dragon Dies

When it finally falls, the chamber convulses. The three planes that have been held partially merged begin to separate — and for a few agonizing seconds, the party exists in all of them at once. The material stone. The Shadowfell darkness pressing in. The wrong-bright Feywild light from somewhere that should not be here. Then the separation completes and they are in the material plane, and the chamber is just a chamber, and the silence is very loud.

Then the party's fragments react — violently, all at once. Each character experiences a vision of the Fugue Plane: a gray city on a featureless plain, thousands of still figures slowly turning to look at them.

The cult now knows exactly who they are and where they are. The Greyhaven arc ends. The larger campaign begins.

Dungeon Master's Note

The dragon is tragic, not evil. It did not choose this. It has been suffering for a very long time, existing as a wound in reality rather than a creature. If the party finds a way to communicate with it before or during combat, they may hear something that makes the fight much harder to finish — a fragment of its actual name, spoken in its own voice, buried under the hundreds of other voices. That is the right feeling. Let them feel the weight of what was done to it.
Campaign Arc
The Mass Grave
What is beneath Greyhaven. Why the dead cannot rest. How it ends.

What Happened Here

Generations ago, the Choir of Last Mercy performed their first attempt at planar collapse at a site outside Greyhaven. They selected this location because the mountains above it sit at a natural confluence of planar proximity — the Feywild is thin here in summer, the Shadowfell is thin here in winter, and the ground has always been slightly colder than it should be. The cult sacrificed hundreds of people simultaneously, using the mass death-event to try to crack the planar boundary at a single point and force the collapse to cascade outward.

It partially worked. The boundary cracked but did not break. The hundreds of souls did not complete their passage to the Fugue — they became trapped between planes, caught in the wound the ritual created. The chamber was sealed with the dragon inside it, holding the partial collapse open, and the grave was buried over the top. The cult moved on, treating Greyhaven as a failed prototype. They have not returned since.

The grave was recently disturbed — grave robbers, most likely, looking for valuables from old burials. They cracked one of the ritual seals without knowing it existed. The wound, which had been contained for generations, began to breathe again. The planes bleed through the crack. The dead catch Shadowfell-echoes instead of their own souls on the way back. The cold that cannot end is planes bleeding into each other: where the material and the Shadowfell overlap, everything is slightly more dead than it should be.

What They Find When They Dig

Below the grave: shattered ritual pillars, carved with runes in a language that feels older than language should be. Bone masks — dozens of them — arranged in concentric circles around a massive cracked altar. The stone around the altar is wrong in a way that takes a moment to name: some of it is too dark, too cold, pressing with the distinctive weight of the Shadowfell. Some of it, in the far corner, has something growing on it that is not moss — something that catches the light with the faint luminescence of deep Feywild. The planes have been bleeding here for generations. It shows. Below the altar, stairs go further down into a chamber that should not exist beneath a small mountain town. At the bottom of those stairs: the dragon.

Why the Party Cannot Simply Leave

Beyond the moral weight of abandoning people they have come to love, the storm itself prevents easy departure — visibility remains too low for safe travel through the mountain passes. The fragments inside the party also grow more active near the grave; leaving the area causes them to flare painfully, as if the Hollow Saint is pulling them back. And practically: the party knows. Leaving means the dead keep rising, the town keeps dying, and Sister Maren keeps standing in the snow asking why she cannot feel her goddess.

Horror Encounters
Psychological beats to seed across sessions. Do not rush these.
Dungeon Master's Note

These encounters are not combat. They are moments of powerlessness. The horror of this campaign works best when the players cannot fight it — when the only thing to do is witness. Use these sparingly, spaced across sessions, always surrounded by warmth on either side.

The Burial That Moves

During a burial service for a minor townsfolk, the dirt on a nearby older grave shifts. Just slightly. As if something beneath is repositioning itself. Nothing emerges. The priest continues the service. No one else seems to notice. Let the players sit with it.

The Frozen Choir

While passing near the graveyard, the party hears singing. Soft. Beautiful. When they investigate:

Dozens of bodies are buried under shallow snow — not quite covered, as if the earth rejected them. Their mouths are open. The wind passing over them creates the sound of singing. It is, technically, just the wind. That does not make it better.

The Man Who Forgot He Was Dead

The party encounters a man wandering outside town. He is cold but responsive. He says he got lost hunting. He is grateful. He comes inside, eats, sits by the fire. Then, later that night, the innkeeper says quietly: that man died two winters ago. That night, in his room, he begins decaying rapidly — still conscious, still confused, still asking what is happening to him.

The Silent Inn Guest

A traveler arrives at the Lantern Rest. He never speaks. He orders nothing. He sits at a corner table and watches the party with focused, patient attention. Late that night, someone notices: he casts no shadow. When confronted, he removes his mask. Beneath it is another mask — this one bone, carved with a peaceful face. He says calmly, without surprise:

"You've grown well."

Then he stands and walks into the storm. He does not run. He does not hurry. He simply goes, and the storm takes him, and he is gone.

The Dead Walking Home

Late in the town arc — after the party understands what is happening but before they have stopped it — they witness this:

Every dead person who has returned is walking. Not toward the party. Not toward the living. Toward the graveyard. Slowly, steadily, all at once, from different parts of town — converging. When stopped and asked where they are going, each one says the same thing: "Something is calling us home."

Sister Maren is among them. Father Elric is not — he is already gone. Tomm is among them, and he turns to look at Visage as he passes, and does not stop walking.

Campaign Arc
Beyond the Town Arc
Where the campaign goes after Greyhaven. What the party carries with them.

What the Dragon's Death Changes

The storm ends — slowly, over days rather than all at once. The planes stop bleeding at Greyhaven. The dead stop rising. The grave rests. The town survives, diminished. The party leaves carrying knowledge that cannot be unfelt: that their own existence is weakening the barriers between worlds, that removing what the cult put inside them would kill them, and that the cult now knows exactly where they are.

The Shape of What Follows

The campaign becomes a race between understanding and collapse. The party will discover:

  • There are other fragment vessels across the world — other people whose planar crossings were exploited, most of them unaware of what they carry
  • The cult is hunting them all, intending to activate as many anchors as possible simultaneously to trigger the cascade collapse
  • Cantors have been following the party since before Greyhaven — since before the party can fully account for
  • Davan's war was connected — the cult has been operating for generations, and some of what the party thought was history is actually infrastructure
  • The Grand Requiem is aware of them specifically, because their fragments have been resonating in ways the cult did not design and cannot fully explain
  • There is a site in the Feywild — connected to Petal's past — that is already another wound like Greyhaven

The Argument the Cult Will Make

Eventually, the cult will stop hiding. A Cantor — or someone higher — will sit down with the party and make their case directly. Calmly. With evidence. They will describe the Wall of the Faithless. They will describe the souls that do not reach peace. They will describe what the Fugue actually is and why they believe it is the only real world. They will not threaten the party. They will try to convince them.

This conversation should be one of the hardest scenes in the campaign to run. The party should not be able to immediately dismiss it. Let it sit in the room like a stone. Let players argue with each other afterward. That argument — whether their world is worth fighting for — is what the campaign is actually about.

The Worst Truth

The party will learn that removing their fragments kills them. They will be searching for another way. The campaign does not promise there is one. It asks: what do you do when you are the problem, and the solution might be your death, and someone is telling you that death is just waking up?

The Final Emotional Question: Through Petal's guilt and her love for a world that exiled her. Through Ardikan's survival of something he didn't earn, and his faith in a god whose answer might be silence. Through Visage's emerging soul — the thing that is making him most human is also the thing that might get him killed by a god who has no record of him. The question is whether love and identity and faith are enough to fight for a world someone tells you isn't real. The answer should be hard-won and cost something real.
